# Iceland Bakery

Iceland's one Costco in **Garðabær** has to compete with traditional Icelandic bakeries — the *bakarí* — that have been perfecting their craft for generations. People have *opinions* about which bakarí makes the best kleinur.

Costco's play is straightforward: take the local classics and do them in bulk. The result is surprisingly good, and **significantly cheaper** than downtown Reykjavík's boutique bakeries. In a country where a single pastry can cost more than a London cappuccino, bulk baking is practically a public service.

## Seasonal

### Christmas (December)

- **Rjómabollur** — Cream puffs with chocolate on top, filled with whipped cream or custard. Peak around *bóndadagur* (Husband's Day) in late January. The 6-pack disappears faster than you'd expect.
- **Jólakaka** — Christmas cake with dried fruit and cardamom. Dense, fragrant, sliced thin with butter.
- **Piparkökur** — Icelandic ginger cookies, thinner and crispier than you'd expect. A Christmas essential.

### Þorrablót (January–February)

- **Pönnukökur** — Thin rolled crêpes filled with cream and jam, or just sugar and butter. One of the milder Þorrablót offerings (no fermented shark here).
- **Increased kleinur stock** — twisted donuts pair well with strong Icelandic coffee during the long, dark afternoons.

### Summer (June–August)

- The bakery leans lighter during Iceland's brief, luminous summer. Vínarbrauð and croissants sell well as Icelanders pack for **Ring Road** trips.
- **Rúgbrauð** makes excellent travel food — dense, shelf-stable, pairs with anything.

## A Note on Prices

Everything costs more on a volcanic island in the North Atlantic — that's just physics and supply chains. Flour is imported, butter is expensive, and sugar doesn't grow in lava fields (yet).

But Costco's bakery remains **one of the cheapest sources of fresh baked goods in Iceland**. A single kleinur at a Reykjavík bakarí runs 300–400 ISK — a 12-pack at Costco for 899 ISK is the kind of math that keeps Icelanders renewing their memberships.

The rúgbrauð is an especially good deal — artisan rye loaves in Reykjavík shops cost 1,500+ ISK. Costco's at 799 ISK won't win a geothermal baking competition, but it'll keep your smoked lamb sandwiches honest.

> **The scene:** On weekends, the bakery counter at Garðabær moves fast. Icelanders know what they want — kleinur, skúffukaka, maybe some vínarbrauð — and they don't linger. The rúgbrauð sells out by early afternoon. Plan accordingly.
