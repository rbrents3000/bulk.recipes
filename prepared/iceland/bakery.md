# Iceland Bakery

Iceland's one Costco in Garðabær has to compete with traditional Icelandic bakeries — the *bakarí* — that have been perfecting their craft for generations. These are institutions. People have *opinions* about which bakarí makes the best kleinur. Costco's play here is straightforward: take the local classics and do them in bulk. The result is surprisingly good, and significantly cheaper than the boutique bakeries in downtown Reykjavík. In a country where a single pastry can cost more than a London cappuccino, bulk baking is practically a public service.

## Seasonal

### Christmas (December)

- **Rjómabollur** — Cream puffs with chocolate on top, filled with whipped cream or custard. These start appearing in December and peak around *bóndadagur* (Husband's Day) in late January, when tradition demands they be eaten in quantity. The Costco version comes in a 6-pack, which sounds like a lot until you realize how fast they disappear.
- **Jólakaka** — Christmas cake studded with dried fruit and spiced with cardamom. Dense, fragrant, and sliced thin with butter. Every Icelandic grandmother has a version; Costco's is sized for the whole extended family.
- **Piparkökur** — Icelandic ginger cookies, thinner and crispier than you'd expect. A Christmas essential, and the subject of fierce decorating competitions in Icelandic households every December.

### Þorrablót (January–February)

- **Pönnukökur** — Thin rolled crêpes filled with cream and jam, or sometimes just sugar and butter. A comfort food for the darkest months, and one of the milder Þorrablót offerings (no fermented shark here, thankfully).
- Increased kleinur stock — twisted donuts pair well with strong Icelandic coffee during the long, dark afternoons. The bakery knows its audience.

### Summer (June–August)

- The bakery leans lighter during Iceland's brief, luminous summer — vínarbrauð and croissants sell well as Icelanders pack for road trips around the Ring Road. The rúgbrauð makes excellent travel food: dense, shelf-stable, pairs with anything.

## A Note on Prices

Everything costs more on a volcanic island in the North Atlantic — that's just physics and supply chains. Flour is imported. Butter is expensive. Sugar doesn't grow in lava fields (yet). But Costco's bakery remains one of the cheapest sources of fresh baked goods in Iceland by a comfortable margin. A single kleinur at a Reykjavík bakarí might run you 300–400 ISK. A 12-pack at Costco for 899 ISK is the kind of math that keeps Icelanders renewing their memberships.

The rúgbrauð is an especially good deal — artisan rye loaves in Reykjavík shops can cost 1,500+ ISK. Costco's version at 799 ISK won't win a geothermal baking competition, but it'll keep your smoked lamb sandwiches honest.

> **The scene:** On weekends, the bakery counter at Garðabær moves fast. Icelanders know what they want — kleinur, skúffukaka, maybe some vínarbrauð — and they don't linger. The rúgbrauð sells out by early afternoon. Plan accordingly.
