# Copycat Costco Japan Takoyaki (Octopus Balls)

> Crispy outside, gooey inside octopus balls — iconic Japanese street food that's absurdly fun to make at home. Costco Japan sells 20+ count for 998 yen; you can do it yourself for about 75 cents a pop.

*Costco Japan sells this as a prepared deli item — here's how to make it at home with Costco US ingredients.*

**Prep:** 20 min | **Cook:** 15 min | **Servings:** 24 pieces | **Cost:** ~$0.75/piece

## Costco Shopping List
- Kirkland Signature Large Eggs — 2 eggs (~$7.99, 5 dozen)
- Octopus (tako) — 1/2 lb, diced into small cubes (~$8.99/lb, from the seafood section)
- Green Onions — 4 stalks, finely chopped (~$1.49, bunch)

## Other Ingredients
- 1 cup all-purpose flour
- 2 cups dashi stock (or 2 cups water + 2 teaspoons dashi powder)
- 1/2 teaspoon salt
- 1 teaspoon soy sauce
- 2 tablespoons pickled red ginger (beni shoga), finely chopped
- 2 tablespoons tenkasu (tempura scraps) *(optional but highly recommended)*
- Takoyaki sauce (or substitute: equal parts Worcestershire and ketchup with a dash of soy sauce)
- Kewpie mayonnaise
- Katsuobushi (bonito flakes)
- Aonori (dried green seaweed flakes)
- Neutral oil for greasing the pan

## Instructions
1. Whisk together flour, eggs, dashi, salt, and soy sauce until you have a thin, smooth batter — think crepe batter, not pancake batter. Let it rest 10 minutes.
2. Heat your takoyaki pan over medium heat and oil each well generously with a brush or paper towel.
3. Pour batter into each well until it just overflows slightly. Drop a few cubes of octopus, a pinch of green onion, pickled ginger, and tenkasu into each well.
4. Cook 2-3 minutes until the bottom sets and turns golden. Use a chopstick or takoyaki pick to rotate each ball 90 degrees, tucking any overflow batter underneath.
5. Keep turning every minute or so until all sides are evenly golden and crispy, about 4-5 minutes total. They should spin freely when done.
6. Transfer to a plate. Drizzle generously with takoyaki sauce and Kewpie mayo. Shower with bonito flakes (watch them dance in the heat) and aonori.

> **Tip:** You absolutely need a takoyaki pan for this — no amount of kitchen improvisation will replicate those perfect spheres. Cast iron ones run about $20 online and last forever. Electric ones with temperature control are even easier if you plan to make these regularly (and you will).

> **Tip:** The batter should be very thin and watery. If it looks like pancake batter, add more dashi. The thin batter is what creates that signature molten, custardy interior.

## Storage
Best eaten immediately — the whole point is that crispy-outside-molten-inside contrast. If you must, refrigerate up to 2 days and reheat in a 400F oven for 5 minutes. Do not microwave unless you enjoy sad, rubbery balls.

## Leftover Ideas
- Toss in a bento box with rice and pickled vegetables
- Flatten leftover batter into a thin okonomiyaki-style pancake with the same toppings
